Energy-first: making a sensor run a year on twenty minutes of sun

Power design ESP32 Sylvara

Most “smart plant” gadgets die the same death: they end up in a drawer because nobody wants to charge a flowerpot every week. When we started Sylvara, that failure mode was the whole problem statement. The device had to disappear into the background and stay alive for a season without anyone thinking about it.

So we inverted the usual order of design. Instead of choosing features and then asking what battery they'd need, we fixed the energy budget first and let it veto everything else.

Start from the energy budget, not the feature list

The target was blunt: multi-month operation in zero sunlight, and effectively unlimited runtime on as little as twenty minutes of sun a day. Once that number is non-negotiable, it makes decisions for you. Every milliamp-hour has to justify itself.

In practice that meant three things became first-class constraints rather than afterthoughts:

  • Sleep is the default state. The MCU — an ESP32-C3 — spends almost all of its life in deep sleep. “Awake” is the exception, measured in milliseconds.
  • Peripherals are guilty until proven innocent. Sensors and radios are power-gated and only energised for the slice of time they're actually sampling or transmitting.
  • Sensing is duty-cycled. Soil moisture doesn't change in seconds. Reading it every few minutes instead of continuously is invisible to the user and enormous for the battery.

Harvest more than you spend

The other half of the equation is income, not just spending. A BQ25798-based power path lets the system charge from a small solar cell, USB-C PD, or both, and sip from a Li-ion cell in between. The goal isn't a huge battery — it's a system where a short daily trickle of sunlight more than covers the day's tiny draw.

If average harvest > average consumption, runtime stops being a number and becomes “indefinite.” That's the line we engineered toward.

Why this shapes the whole product

Designing energy-first doesn't only change the battery — it ripples outward. Component selection bends toward low quiescent current. Firmware scheduling becomes a question of “what's the cheapest way to learn this?” Even the communication strategy — when to talk to the cloud, and how little to say — falls out of the power budget.

The payoff is a device you can install and forget, which for an unattended field sensor is the entire value proposition. Reliability and “I never have to think about it” are the same feature.

Takeaway

If a project lives or dies on battery life, don't treat power as a spec to hit at the end. Make it the constraint that everything else negotiates with. It's a harder way to start — and a much easier device to live with.
